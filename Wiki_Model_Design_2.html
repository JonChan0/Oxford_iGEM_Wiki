Part III: Spatial Mathematical Model

Consider the following spatial outline of C. difficile being upregulated in the intestine.

As MCell takes many computations into account such as diffusion and probabilistic simulations, it would require an unrealistic amount of computational power to model a truly realistic theoretical model at the correct timescale without any assumptions.

Assumptions

To obtain qualitative results from the spatial simulations, we made the following assumptions:

  • As the size of bacterial cells is minute relative to the intestine, we can ignore the curvature of the intestinal wall upon which the bacterial cells thrive.
  • Kinetic rate constants are estimated such that 90% of the C. difficile population would be upregulated by 2-3 AIP molecules per cell.
  • The efficiency of the AgrAC two-component regulatory system in our L. reuteri would be equal to that in C. difficile
  • Upregulated C.difficile does not produce AIP molecules.

Discussion

How can our system thus cope with the signal starvation evident?

Simply strengthening the promoter to increase the number of AgrC receptors could theoretically cause upregulation to occur at lower concentrations of AIP, but it does not address the issue of the system failing in different spatial orientations in the gut.

A potential solution is a secondary amplification system that uses a separate signalling molecule in our system. Theoretically, It would mean that a small fraction of the L. reuteri being upregulated could trigger an upregulation cascade that up-regulates our system sufficiently rapidly.

Part IV: Secondary Amplification System

How then can we beat C. difficile at its own upregulation game?

One way is for the engineered L. reuteri cells to upregulate other L. reuteri cells once a small fraction of the population has been upregulated by C. difficile-regulated AIP. This can be accomplished by incorporating an additional signal secretion & detection system - a secondary amplification system.

To observe the efficacy of our engineered L. reuteri with a hypothetical secondary amplification system, we incorporated new differential equations into our existing deterministic mathematical model here and simulated this new integrated system.


Secondary Amplification System Primer

For our generic secondary amplification system, consider a signal X and its complementary receptor Y which can only be produced by previously AgrA/C-upregulated L. reuteri cells. Such a signal X is assumed to secreted and then bind with membrane receptor Y in neighbouring L. reuteri cells to form complex XY. This complex thus acts as a transcription factor that upregulates the L. reuteri cell.
