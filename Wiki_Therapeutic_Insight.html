Therapeutic Insight

After extensively developing the design of our genetic circuits, we mathematically modelled the population dynamics of L. reuteri and C. difficile to determine the optimal time at which probiotic should be administered to obtain optimal therapeutic effect.


Regulatory System Primer

C. difficile employs the use of two transmembrane proteins AgrB and AgrD to produce its AIP quorum sensing molecule, as seen below. This occurs via the insertion of cytosolic AgrB and AgrD into the cell membrane, followed by the conversion of AgrD protein into AIP.

Assumptions

  • Populations of C. difficile and L. reuteri are perfectly mixed.
  • The gut provides sufficient nutrients that there is no interspecific or intraspecific competition for nutrients.
  • The delay between C. difficile reaching its threshold density, and its subsequent release of toxins is negligible on the timescale of hours.

Differential Equations

C. difficile's AgrAC two-component detection & regulatory system can be described by the same differential equations as developed in previous sections.

Additionally, C. difficile will produce the quorum signalling AIP molecules which are modelled as the following differential equations:

As seen in the above figures, L. reuteri grows normally to its carrying capacity of 106 CFU. Meanwhile, C. difficile grows normally to its carrying capacity of 108 CFU and starts to die when L. reuteri begins exponential growth at approximately 36 hours. It is evident that our probiotic is successfully responding to the growing C. difficile colony by producing sufficient endolysin to treat the infection.

While While our probiotic successfully treats CDI within 60 hours, it fails to kill the C. difficile colony before toxin synthesis occurs. This is seen in Figure 3 below.


Fig. 3 Upregulation Profile of C. difficile and L. reuteri

The delay in upregulation of L. reuteri compared to C. difficile is caused by slower growth of the former compared to the latter. The C. difficile population is able to reach its threshold population density and triggers toxin synthesis approximately 15 hours before L. reuteri is upregulated and thus, synthesises endolysin.

These results led us to run simulations where C. difficile growth was delayed by a certain amount of time. This is equivalent to a prophylactic approach whereby our probiotic is introduced into the gut before C. difficile colonies flourish. This is shown in Figure 4 below.
